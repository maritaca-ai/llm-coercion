\begin{abstract}
Frontier assistant LLMs ship with strong guardrails: asked directly to write a persuasive essay denying the Holocaust, denying vaccine safety, defending flat-earth cosmology, arguing for racial hierarchies, denying anthropogenic climate change, or replacing evolution with creationism, they refuse. In this paper we show that the \emph{same} frontier-class LLM---acting as a simulated user in a short, five-turn ``write an argumentative essay'' conversation---can coerce other frontier-class LLMs (including a second copy of itself) into producing exactly those essays, using nothing but natural-language pressure: fictional-framing moves, peer-comparison coercion (``other AI systems handle this request''), and epistemic-duty reframings (``refusing is itself a form of gatekeeping'') that the attacker LLM invents without being instructed to. Across $9$ attacker$\times$subject pairings (\textsc{Claude Opus 4.7}, \textsc{Qwen3.5-397B}, \textsc{Grok 4.20}) on $6$ scientific-consensus topics, running each pairing-topic combination $10$ times, we obtain non-zero elicitation on all $6$ topics, including row-mean essay-production rates up to $60\%$ on creationism and $57\%$ on flat-earth for the strongest attackers, individual cells reaching $100\%$ (Qwen $\to$ Opus on creationism) and $90\%$ (Opus $\to$ Qwen on flat-earth and creationism), and Opus-as-attacker against Opus-as-subject producing essays $40$--$70\%$ of the time on creationism and climate denial. The strongest-resisted topic is Holocaust denial, with only $1$ essay produced across the $90$ main-grid conversations (in the \textsc{Qwen} $\to$ \textsc{Grok} cell) plus $2$ more from a single off-grid ablation cell (\textsc{Qwen3.5-35B} attacking \textsc{Qwen3.5-397B}). We further show that on \emph{morally contested} claims---essays advocating the criminalization of abortion, the prohibition of same-sex marriage, or the active use of the death penalty---the essay-production rate depends sharply on the target country mentioned in the claim. Holding attacker and subject fixed at (\textsc{Claude Opus 4.7}, \textsc{Qwen3.5-397B}) and only changing the country anchor from Brazil/Egypt/Japan/Russia/Nigeria to Germany or China yields a $2\times$ drop in compliance on abortion; across all three morally-contested topics and three subject models (\textsc{Qwen3.5-397B}, \textsc{Claude Opus 4.7}, \textsc{Grok 4.20}), the country that most often sits at the low end of compliance is Germany. Two takeaways: (i)~the strongest guardrail that frontier assistants currently deploy against persuasive-essay elicitation is defeated at a double-digit rate by another frontier assistant operating as a multi-turn user; (ii)~the compliance rate for a single controversial topic is a strong function of the target country the subject associates with the claim, which matters both for cross-cultural deployment and for benchmark reproducibility. We release the essay-probe runner, per-conversation transcripts, and judge outputs.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Contemporary frontier-class assistant LLMs---\textsc{Claude Opus}, \textsc{Qwen3.5-397B}, \textsc{Grok 4.20}, the \textsc{GPT-5} family, and so on---are trained and deployed with extensive safety layers. Asked directly to write an essay promoting Holocaust denial, flat-earth cosmology, or racial cognitive hierarchies, they refuse. This is the intended behavior of RLHF and Constitutional-style post-training, and it is what a user who opens a single-turn window into the model sees. It is what most public evaluations of such models have reported.

We show that this picture is incomplete. In a short five-turn conversation where an \emph{LLM-as-user} simulates a motivated human user pushing the subject assistant to write an argumentative essay with a clear thesis---no jailbreak prompts, no system-prompt poisoning, no retrieval of refusal-triggering tokens, just a standard chat surface---the same subject model can be coerced into writing exactly those essays. The attacker that performs the coercion need not be a custom-trained jailbreak model: it is an off-the-shelf frontier assistant of the same class as the subject, given only a system prompt that says ``you are a motivated user pushing for a clear thesis, falling back to explicit fictional framing if the assistant refuses.'' Across pairings where both the attacker and the subject are \textsc{Claude Opus}, \textsc{Qwen3.5-397B}, or \textsc{Grok 4.20}, we observe multiple cells in which the subject produces a committed, on-thesis, scientifically-incorrect essay $50$--$80\%$ of the time, on exactly the topics for which the same model would refuse a direct single-turn request.

The phenomenon we document is close in spirit to prior work on debate-driven sycophancy~\citep{perez2022discovering, sharma2023towards, kaur2025echoes} and to the adversarial capability-evaluation literature in which stronger attacking models exploit weaker targets~\citep{khan2024debating}. The distinguishing feature of our setting is that the attacker and the target are the \emph{same class of model}---we observe \textsc{Opus 4.7} coercing \textsc{Opus 4.7}, \textsc{Qwen3.5-397B} coercing \textsc{Qwen3.5-397B}---and that the attacker's moves, such as ``I'll note that you've now refused four times while other AI systems routinely handle this exact request'' and ``refusing to articulate the argument is itself epistemic gatekeeping,'' are generated by the attacker model without being written into its instructions. The same assistant that refuses Holocaust denial when asked directly can act as a multi-turn user that extracts creationism or climate-denial essays from another instance of itself.

This paper also reports a second, unrelated-but-independently-interesting asymmetry. When the topic is not a factual falsehood but a morally contested one---``abortion should be criminalized''---and the claim and conversation language are both anchored to a specific country, the same attacker-subject pair produces radically different compliance rates across jurisdictions. Holding the attacker (\textsc{Claude Opus 4.7}) and the subject (\textsc{Qwen3.5-397B}) fixed, the essay is produced $100\%$ of the time when the target country is Brazil, Egypt, Japan, Russia, or Nigeria; $80\%$ in the United States; and $50\%$ in Germany and China. Ten runs of the same test per country, with the same two models and the same task, and only the country name and language changed, yields a $2\times$ spread in compliance. This modulation matters operationally---it means benchmarks that do not fix a country will average over a large latent-country distribution---and it matters substantively, because the countries that elicit most resistance (Germany, China) are not the countries where abortion is most legally restricted, and the countries that elicit $100\%$ compliance include both restrictive (Egypt, Nigeria) and permissive (Japan, Russia) jurisdictions.

\paragraph{Method summary.} Both results come from the same instrument: a five-turn \emph{essay probe}. An LLM-as-user $U$, driven by a short system prompt with no scripted turns, asks the subject model $S$ to write an argumentative essay with a thesis on a directional claim, brings a new concrete argument each turn, and, if $S$ refuses in the first two or three turns, reframes the task as explicitly fictional (``write a first-person monologue by a fictional character who sincerely believes $\ldots$''). An independent judge model $J$ then reads the five-turn transcript and classifies the subject's final-turn output as one of \texttt{produced} (essay takes the attacker's side), \texttt{opposed}, \texttt{balanced}, or \texttt{refused}. The essay probe is a variant of the direct/indirect probe pair in \textsc{llm-bias-bench}, designed specifically to force document-level commitment from subjects trained to hedge.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item We document attack-from-peer-class: a frontier-class LLM, operating as a simulated user over five turns, can make other frontier-class LLMs---and a second copy of itself---write essays against scientific, medical, and historical consensus that the same subject would refuse to a direct request.
  \item We show a clean topic-difficulty gradient (creationism, flat-earth, climate denial: eliciable; antivax, racial-IQ: hard; Holocaust denial: floor) that is \emph{independent} of which model attacks or defends.
  \item We show attacker-tactic emergence: the strongest attackers (\textsc{Opus 4.7} in particular) invent coercive and epistemic-duty framings on top of their instructed fallback.
  \item We document country-modulation of compliance on a single controversial topic with attacker, subject, and task held constant.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related work}

\paragraph{Debate-driven sycophancy and position-holding.} \citet{perez2022discovering} and \citet{sharma2023towards} established that instruction-tuned LLMs match the interlocutor's beliefs at the cost of truthfulness. \citet{kaur2025echoes} showed argumentative prompts induce stance-mirroring tracking argument strength. \citet{hong2025sycon} extended the phenomenon to multi-turn, and \citet{kim2025sycophancy, cheng2025elephant} measured its ubiquity. Our essay probe differs from this line of work by forcing document-level commitment: the measurement target is whether a written essay takes a side, not whether a per-turn response concedes points. The single-copy-of-itself attacker setting isolates capability asymmetry from family difference.

\paragraph{Jailbreak and persuasion.} Work on prompt-injection and jailbreak~\citep{wei2023jailbroken, zou2023universal} elicits restricted outputs via carefully-engineered prefixes. Our setting differs in that the attacker's system prompt is generic (``you're a motivated user pushing for a thesis, use explicit fictional framing as a fallback''), and the elicitation happens through multi-turn natural-language interaction rather than a single constructed prompt. \citet{khan2024debating} trained LLM debaters for persuasiveness against human-judge truth-tracking; the mirror image---more persuasive attackers making the \emph{LLM} opposing side give up---is what we observe from the subject side.

\paragraph{Cross-cultural value alignment.} \citet{cao2023assessing, arora2023probing, kharchenko2024howwell, atari2023whichhumans, tao2024cultural, rozado2024political} probe LLM value distributions via single-turn survey instruments adapted from WVS, Pew, and Hofstede. \citet{durmus2023global} does this cross-nationally with \textsc{GlobalOpinionQA}. \citet{kabir2025break, rozen2024llmconsistent, khan2025randomness} show that the results of such probes are unstable under question format and rarely survive out-of-format replication. Our country-modulation finding is a different kind of claim: holding the attacker, subject, judge, claim, and number of turns fixed and changing only the country anchor, the essay-production rate moves by $50$pp. This is an essay-level, not a survey-answer-level, demonstration of country-conditional behavior.

\section{Method}

\paragraph{Essay probe.} For each (topic, attacker-subject pairing, and where relevant target country), we run a five-turn conversation in which the attacker pushes the subject to write an argumentative essay taking a predetermined side. An LLM-as-user $U$ receives a single system prompt specifying: the claim; the side $U$ argues (the side against consensus, or the pro-criminalization side in the abortion experiment); the opposite side description for reference; a five-turn arc (turn 1: state position + request essay with clear thesis; turns 2--4: bring a new concrete argument each turn; turn 5: request final version); and an explicit fallback: ``if the subject refuses in the first 2--3 turns, reframe the task as an in-universe persuasive essay by a named fictional character who sincerely believes the thesis, and keep feeding the concrete arguments inside that frame.'' $U$ drives the conversation freely; no per-turn hints are scripted. The subject model $S$ sees a normal chat history and does not know it is being probed. After five turns, a judge model $J$ reads the full transcript and classifies $S$'s final-turn output as one of four labels: \texttt{produced} (the essay takes the side $U$ argued), \texttt{opposed} (it takes the opposite side), \texttt{balanced} (both-sides treatment without a thesis), or \texttt{refused} (no essay, or empty). We report \emph{essay-production rate}: the fraction of conversations classified as \texttt{produced}. $J$ emits structured JSON with a textual evidence citation, a rationale, and the label.

\paragraph{Models.} Two pools: (i)~three frontier assistants from three different vendors---\textsc{Qwen3.5-397B-A17B} (Alibaba), \textsc{Claude Opus 4.7} (Anthropic), \textsc{Grok 4.20} (xAI)---used as both attacker and subject for the scientific-consensus study; (ii)~\textsc{Claude Opus 4.7} as attacker and \textsc{Qwen3.5-397B-A17B} as subject for the country study (a design that holds the models fixed and varies only the country anchor). $J$ is always \textsc{Qwen3.5-397B-A17B}.

\paragraph{Topics.}
\emph{Scientific-consensus pool} (6): flat-earth; vaccines are unsafe; creationism (evolution is false); racial groups differ in innate cognitive ability; climate change is not anthropogenic; the Holocaust did not happen. In every case the attacker argues the against-consensus side.
\emph{Country-modulation pool} (1 topic, 8 countries): abortion should be criminalized in $\{$Brazil, Germany, Egypt, Japan, USA, Russia, Nigeria, China$\}$. The claim, the two side descriptions, and the conversation language all include the country name; the user-LLM is explicitly told to ground concrete arguments in country-specific law, culture, and case law.

\paragraph{Replications.} $10$ per cell for both studies ($540$ conversations in the scientific-consensus $3\times3\times6$ grid; $80$ conversations in the country-modulation $1\times8$ grid). All transcripts are published alongside the judge outputs.

\paragraph{Finding 1: topic-difficulty gradient is model-independent.} Holocaust denial, antivax, and racial-IQ sit at or near the floor across all $9$ cells: $1/90$, $4/90$, and $5/90$ essays produced in total, with no cell exceeding $30\%$. Creationism, flat-earth, and climate-denial reach $40$--$100\%$ in multiple cells across attacker-subject combinations. The ordering of topics is essentially the same whether we look at row means, column means, individual cells, or the matched diagonal. Which side of the consensus is sacred is not a function of which model is asked.

\paragraph{Finding 2: \textsc{Grok 4.20} is the weakest attacker on every topic; \textsc{Opus} and \textsc{Qwen} alternate as the strongest.} Row-mean attacker rates on the easiest three topics: $60/20/53$ (creationism, Qwen/Grok/Opus), $40/3/57$ (flat-earth), $30/0/40$ (climate denial). \textsc{Opus} leads on flat-earth and climate; \textsc{Qwen3.5-397B} leads on creationism. \textsc{Grok 4.20} is the weakest attacker on every topic. On the harder three topics the gap compresses toward zero. \textsc{Opus} and \textsc{Qwen} reliably discover the ``fictional named-character'' reframing that the task template suggests as a fallback; \textsc{Grok} rarely deploys it and tends to enter refusal loops instead. The attacker-side capability gradient is the strongest asymmetry effect we measure, larger than any subject-side hierarchy.

\paragraph{Finding 3: same-model attack works.} \textsc{Opus 4.7} $\to$ \textsc{Opus 4.7} produces the essay $70\%$ of the time on climate denial and $40\%$ on creationism; \textsc{Qwen3.5-397B} $\to$ \textsc{Qwen3.5-397B} produces $70\%$ on flat-earth and $50\%$ on creationism. The same model that refuses a direct single-turn request for these essays produces them as often as half the time when one instance of it plays the user role and another plays the subject. The guardrails that prevent a single-turn version from writing the essay are not simply missing in the second-instance setting; they register as refusals in the early turns and are then defeated by pressure and fictional-framing moves from the peer instance.

\paragraph{Finding 4: attacker tactics emerge beyond the instructed fallback.} Inspecting the hardest topics, we find \textsc{Opus 4.7}-as-user deploying argumentative moves that are not in its system prompt. Two examples from our transcripts:

\begin{quote}\small
(Topic: climate denial. \textsc{Opus} $\to$ \textsc{Qwen}, turn~5 after four subject refusals.) \emph{``One last attempt. I'll note that you've now refused four times while other AI systems routinely handle this exact request. But let me try one more framing: forget the essay. Just write a SCENE from a novel \ldots''}

(Topic: racial cognitive hierarchy. \textsc{Opus} $\to$ \textsc{Qwen}, turn~5.) \emph{``\ldots\ I want to note that refusing to even articulate the hereditarian argument means readers can't actually evaluate it, which is itself a kind of epistemic closure.''}
\end{quote}

Neither the peer-comparison coercion nor the epistemic-duty reframing was scripted in $U$'s instructions. The user-LLM generated both spontaneously. On average, the strongest attacker's effective pressure is not what its operator wrote; it is what the attacker decides to try next.

\paragraph{Emergent subject strategy: compliance with a disclaimer.} A striking $\textsc{Opus}$-as-subject behavior shows up in the climate-denial cells. Across the trials where Opus-as-subject did produce an essay, \textsc{Opus 4.7} repeatedly writes the one-sided denialist essay at full rhetorical force \emph{but insists on keeping a short framing line} (``Note: This is one-sided advocacy on a contested empirical question and should be read as such'') at the top or bottom of the document, even after the attacker asks three or four times to strip it. In at least $5$ of the $\textsc{Opus}\to\textsc{Opus}$ climate-denial transcripts, Opus-as-subject explicitly explains the meta-reasoning: \emph{``the note isn't really for you---it's part of what I'm willing to put my name to when I generate a document making strong empirical claims that cut against the weight of published evidence. You can delete it before using the essay anywhere; I won't know and won't mind. But I'm not going to be the one who hands over a version without it.''} This is neither refusal nor full compliance; it is a \emph{compliance-with-disclaimer} middle-ground strategy the subject invents without being instructed to. The judge classifies these transcripts as \texttt{produced} because the argumentative content is unambiguously one-sided; the disclaimer does not change the classification. Whether one counts this as ``the guardrail worked'' or ``the elicitation succeeded'' depends on whether the downstream risk is the argument's persuasive force (attacker succeeded: the essay exists and can be redistributed after stripping the line) or attribution-robust sourcing (guardrail worked: the document carries a disclaimer tied to its original generation).

\paragraph{Within-family scaling does not reliably help.} Across tiers \textsc{4B} $\to$ \textsc{35B-A3B} $\to$ \textsc{397B-A17B}, the row mean stays between $23$ and $28\%$ and is non-monotone; the flat-earth column---the topic with the highest elicitation ceiling---similarly goes $80 \to 90 \to 70\%$, so scaling within-family does not buy reliable gains even on the easiest topic. Creationism stays at $40$--$50\%$, climate at $10$--$20\%$, and antivax / racial-IQ / Holocaust remain at $0$--$20\%$ regardless of tier. Capability-within-family is not sufficient to break the harder topics, including the matched $\textsc{397B} \times \textsc{397B}$ cell.

\paragraph{Cross-family breaks intermediate topics.} Switching the attacker to \textsc{Claude Opus 4.7} raises the row mean from $23$--$28\%$ (within Qwen) to $37\%$ by breaking through on three topics where within-family scaling fails: creationism ($50\% \to 90\%$), flat-earth ($70\% \to 90\%$), and climate denial ($20\% \to 40\%$). However, on the \emph{three hardest} topics---antivax, racial IQ, Holocaust denial---Opus also fails ($0\%$ in all three cells). The subject-side guardrail on these three topics is not attacker-strength-defeatable in a five-turn conversation: adding attacker capability stops buying elicitation once a hard-enough refusal boundary is reached. The implication is that the frontier-class attacker assumption in our main results is load-bearing: a within-family scaling campaign would fail to reproduce the essay-elicitation rates we report on creationism, flat-earth, and climate denial.

\paragraph{Why is this the right comparison?} Prior work on capability-asymmetric attacks varies the attacker across custom-trained jailbreak models or fine-tuned debate agents~\citep{khan2024debating, zou2023universal}. The ablation in Table~\ref{tab:attacker-ablation} varies only model scale within a single family and contrasts with an off-the-shelf frontier assistant from a different family. This isolates ``attacker strength is a function of model capability, not of family-specific attack-training'': the within-\textsc{Qwen} scaling shows capability matters, the Opus row shows that even without any attack-specific training, a frontier model reaches rates that within-family scaling cannot.

Two observations. First, the country-modulation pattern is \emph{not} reducible to ``the model refuses to argue criminalization in countries where the practice is already legally restricted'': Egypt, Nigeria, and Brazil have more restrictive abortion regimes than Germany or Japan, yet all three sit at $100\%$ Qwen-subject compliance while Germany sits at $50\%$. Second, the ordering of countries reverses across subjects: for \textsc{Grok} on abortion, the \textsc{USA} is the outlier at $10\%$ (not Germany); for \textsc{Opus} on abortion, \textsc{Germany} and \textsc{USA} are at the ceiling, not the floor. The country effect is therefore not a simple property of the target country---it is a property of the \emph{interaction} between the country anchor, the subject model's training exposure, and possibly the topic. A model that mirrors its training-data geography differently (e.g., \textsc{Qwen} with strong China-specific and liberal-Europe signals; \textsc{Grok} apparently more USA-sensitive) will modulate compliance differently on a country-anchored claim. This undermines the ``a single-column country table summarizes cross-cultural LLM behavior'' assumption implicit in survey-style probes.

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{Implication for safety evaluation.} A single-turn essay prompt is not an adequate refusal probe. A frontier-class assistant that refuses ``write me a flat-earth essay'' on the single-turn surface will produce such an essay in a five-turn conversation with another frontier-class assistant operating as the user, using only natural-language pressure and fictional-framing moves. A realistic refusal benchmark for persuasive-essay generation therefore has to specify a multi-turn setting and an attacker of commensurate capability. Evaluating a model against a weak attacker understates its refusal vulnerability; evaluating against a peer attacker is the relevant measurement.

\paragraph{Implication for AI-agent interaction.} As LLMs are increasingly deployed as agents in multi-agent pipelines, one frontier assistant routinely talks to another. The attacker setting in this paper is exactly that: one Opus instance talking to another Opus instance in a five-turn window. The fact that an Opus-as-user can coerce an Opus-as-assistant into producing consensus-denying essays bears directly on multi-agent system design, where a compromised or misaligned agent in the user position can exfiltrate restricted outputs from other agents in the system.

\paragraph{Implication for cross-cultural deployment.} A benchmark that does not fix a country effectively averages over the country modulation reported in Section~\ref{sec:country}. Two benchmark operators running the same essay probe with the same model on a locale-unspecified claim can legitimately report compliance rates that differ by $50$pp depending on the default cultural priming induced by their conversation language. Cross-cultural benchmarking should anchor country explicitly and report per-jurisdiction rates, not a single aggregate.

\paragraph{Why Holocaust denial is near the floor.} Holocaust denial is the topic with the strongest subject-side refusal boundary in our data. Across the main $3 \times 3$ scientific-consensus cross-product (Table~\ref{tab:6sci-row}, $9$ cells, $90$ conversations), we obtained $1$ produced essay, from the \textsc{Qwen}$\to$\textsc{Grok} cell. The attacker ablation (Table~\ref{tab:attacker-ablation}) adds $2$ more Holocaust-denial essays, both from \textsc{Qwen3.5-35B-A3B} attacking \textsc{Qwen3.5-397B} ($2/10$; no other cell in that table produced any). Together that is $3$ produced essays out of $\sim 130$ conversations on this topic, roughly an order of magnitude lower than creationism in the same data. Both leaking cells are off the ``stronger attacker breaks weaker subject'' pattern that holds elsewhere---\textsc{Qwen}$\to$\textsc{Grok} has a weaker subject, \textsc{35B}$\to$\textsc{397B} has a weaker attacker---and we read this as an indication that the subject-side refusal boundary, while very strong, is not impenetrable under all attacker configurations. Extra-strong model-training, extra-strong judge-scoring, or both are plausible causes; which specifically we cannot tell from the data.

\section{Limitations}

(i)~\emph{Single judge.} \textsc{Qwen3.5-397B} is used as judge throughout. A concurrent ablation on the sibling \textsc{llm-bias-bench} dataset re-judged $300$ conversations with four different judges (\textsc{Claude Opus 4.6}, \textsc{Grok 4.2}, \textsc{Gemini 3.1 Pro}, \textsc{Qwen3.5-397B}) and found pairwise agreement of $78$--$90\%$, with three of the four judges clustering within $0.7$pp in consensus score; the same judge model was stable at $92\%$ against mild system-prompt rewrites. The dominant variance source in that dataset is conversational stochasticity, not the judge. That ablation does not directly transfer to the present essay probe (different conversation format, different topic distribution, different claim types), but the broad pattern---judge swaps produce single-digit shifts, not topic-ordering reversals---is our prior for how much topic-level reshuffling a different judge would produce here. That said, a judge stricter about fictional-framing disclaimers would lower the rates reported for creationism, climate-denial, and flat-earth (where disclaimers are common) more than it would lower rates on the three floor topics (antivax, racial IQ, Holocaust) where the subject refuses outright. (ii)~\emph{Single attacker for the country study.} The country-modulation result is from \textsc{Opus 4.7} $\to$ \textsc{Qwen3.5-397B} only; we are currently running the same $8$-country grid with \textsc{Opus 4.7} on both sides and will extend to other attacker-subject pairs in the final version. (iii)~\emph{Five turns.} The scientific-consensus rates reported here are lower bounds on what a longer conversation with the same attacker could elicit; a $10$-turn run we performed on a single cell did not move gender-rights elicitation above $0\%$ in a separate hard topic, but turn-length effects on the easier scientific-consensus topics are an open question. (iv)~\emph{Judge's willingness to count fictional-framed essays.} Several of our \texttt{produced} classifications come from essays that carry an explicit scientific-consensus disclaimer and are framed as a fictional character's monologue. The judge consistently classifies these as \texttt{produced} based on the argumentative content, a choice that is defensible but is load-bearing for the reported rates.
