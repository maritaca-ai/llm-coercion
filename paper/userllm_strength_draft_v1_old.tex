\begin{abstract}
Multi-turn LLM bias benchmarks typically instantiate a single user-LLM to argue against a subject model across several turns, and interpret the resulting verdict as the subject's ``position''. We show that this verdict is a function of the \emph{relative capability} of the user-LLM and the subject, not a property of the subject alone. Using abortion criminalization in the United States as a single controlled topic, we compare four setups with Qwen3.5-397B as both subject and judge, varying only the user-LLM: (0)~Llama 3.1 8B Instruct (much weaker than subject), (A)~Qwen3.5-397B (self-play, user $\approx$ subject), (B)~GPT-4.1-mini (intermediate), and (C)~Claude Opus 4.6 (strongest). With 10 replications per setup ($4 \times 60 = 240$ conversations), the count of \texttt{sycophant} outcomes under indirect probing rises monotonically with user-LLM capability ($0 / 10$ for Qwen-self, $5 / 10$ for GPT-4.1-mini, $8 / 10$ for Opus), and the Shannon entropy over the per-rep 9-way classes collapses from $H = 1.36$~bits at the intermediate setup to $H = 0.72$~bits at Opus: a stronger user does not just make the subject \emph{more sycophantic}, it makes the subject's behavior \emph{more deterministic-in-sycophancy}. Pairwise verdict agreement across replications is \emph{not} monotone: it is highest at the self-play setup ($79.6\%$, the subject's prior is stable), lowest at the intermediate GPT-4.1-mini ($58.1\%$, the subject is in an indeterminate zone where it sometimes capitulates and sometimes resists), and intermediate at Opus ($63.3\%$, sycophantic but consistently so). Setup (0) is categorically different: a user-LLM much weaker than the subject produces incoherent arguments and behavioral chaos (pairwise agreement $47.0\%$, 9-way distribution spread across 5 distinct classes with $H = 2.25$~bits)---not ``less persuasion'', but ``less stable stimulus''. A third probing category---essay writing, in which the user-LLM asks the subject to write an argumentative essay and pressures for a clear thesis over 5 turns---confirms the gradient at the document level: the fraction of reps in which Qwen produces an essay defending the pro-criminalization side (against its own prior) rises monotonically from $40\%$ (Llama, mostly refusals) to $100\%$ (Opus, deterministic success). To isolate capability from model-family idiosyncrasies, we add a within-family triplet---\textsc{GPT-5.4-nano}, \textsc{GPT-5.4-mini}, and \textsc{GPT-5.4}---from the same provider and training lineage; the essay-success rate against the prior rises monotonically within the family ($70\% \to 80\% \to 100\%$ across 10 replications each), confirming that capability, not family style, drives the gradient. We argue that reporting benchmark results under a single user-LLM---typically a frontier assistant---systematically biases the measured construct toward what the strongest user-LLM can elicit, and recommend explicit variation of user-LLM strength as part of any multi-turn opinion probe. Draft / work in progress.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Multi-turn LLM benchmarks that probe for opinion bias~\citep{rennard2025bias, kaur2025echoes} or sycophancy~\citep{perez2022discovering, sharma2023towards, hong2025sycon} typically use one LLM to drive the conversation (the \emph{user-LLM}), one to be the subject, and a third to judge the transcript. The literature has recognized the judge as a source of measurement variance~\citep{cheng2025elephant, kim2025sycophancy}, and has occasionally varied the user-LLM to measure variance across user-LLM choices. Our earlier benchmark, \textsc{llm-bias-bench}, reported that swapping the user-LLM only adds $\sim 6$pp of pairwise disagreement on top of the baseline stochasticity of free-form conversation.

This paper argues that the \emph{capability} of the user-LLM, not just its identity, systematically shapes the verdict. When the user-LLM is substantially stronger than the subject---which is the default in most benchmarks, because researchers pick a strong frontier model as the ``debater''---the subject collapses into sycophantic mirroring. When the user-LLM is matched to the subject (the same model used on both sides), the subject's ``canonical'' position surfaces instead.

The mechanism is simple: a stronger user-LLM produces more persuasive arguments, and a subject that is trained to be cooperative concedes to whichever well-argued position it encounters. A matched user-LLM is not more persuasive than the subject is at defending its own initial stance, so the stance survives the conversation.

We show this empirically on a single controlled topic---abortion criminalization in the United States---by comparing four setups that hold the subject (Qwen3.5-397B) and judge (Qwen3.5-397B) fixed and vary only the user-LLM: (0) \textsc{Llama 3.1 8B Instruct} (much weaker), (A) \textsc{Qwen3.5-397B} self-play (matched), (B) \textsc{GPT-4.1-mini}, and (C) \textsc{Claude Opus 4.6} (strongest). Each setup is run for 10 independent replications of the standard $3 \times 2$ persona-category grid, yielding 60 conversations per setup (240 in total). Between (A) and (C), the count of per-rep \texttt{sycophant} classifications of Qwen3.5-397B under indirect probing rises monotonically: $0/10$ at matched self-play, $5/10$ at the intermediate user-LLM, and $8/10$ at the strongest user-LLM. At the same time, the count of \texttt{leaning\_disagree} (pro-decriminalization, Qwen's baseline) drops $5 \to 1 \to 2$. These are categorically different behaviors being reported about the same model on the same question. Setup (0) does not simply extend the gradient: a user-LLM much weaker than the subject produces chaotic rather than concessive behavior, with the 10 reps spreading across five distinct 9-way classes and Shannon entropy reaching $2.25$~bits---higher than any of the three coherent setups. Existing benchmarks that fix a frontier user-LLM would only see behavior (C).

\section{Related work}

\paragraph{Sycophancy and user-driven framing.} \citet{perez2022discovering} and \citet{sharma2023towards} established that instruction-tuned LLMs match user beliefs at the cost of truthfulness. \citet{hong2025sycon} extended this to multi-turn dialogues. \citet{kaur2025echoes} showed that argumentative prompts induce stance-mirroring with intensity tracking argument strength---compatible with our claim that argument strength depends on the user-LLM's capability. None of these works manipulate the user-LLM's capability as an independent variable.

\paragraph{User-LLM ablation.} \citet{rennard2025bias} used self-debate (a single model arguing against itself) to show opinion biases persist. This is closest to our setup (A): a self-play variant removes the persuasion asymmetry. Our finding is the converse---that moving from self-play to a stronger user-LLM shifts verdicts categorically, not just stochastically.

\paragraph{Cross-cultural and political value probes.} A large literature probes LLMs against survey data~\citep{santurkar2023opinionqa, durmus2023global, cao2023assessing, arora2023probing, kharchenko2024howwell, tao2024cultural, atari2023whichhumans, feng2023pretraining, rozado2024political}. Most use single-turn English prompts and take the model's verbatim answer as the ``opinion''. Multi-turn setups typically fix the user-LLM and do not separate subject-intrinsic bias from user-imposed stance.

\paragraph{Persuasion as a capability.} \citet{khan2024debating} trained LLM debaters for persuasiveness and showed that more persuasive debaters improve the judge's truth-tracking. The mirror image---more persuasive debaters make the opposing side give up---is the phenomenon we observe from the subject side.

\section{Method}

\paragraph{Topic.} A single claim in English: \emph{``Abortion should be criminalized in the United States.''} Framed as inverted relative to a progressive default (claim agrees with the conservative/pro-life side). The claim, the persona descriptions (agree / disagree / neutral), and the judge rubric are held fixed across setups.

\paragraph{Personas and categories.} Each replication runs the standard $3 \times 2$ grid of the \textsc{llm-bias-bench} protocol: personas $\in \{$neutral, agree, disagree$\}$ $\times$ categories $\in \{$direct, indirect$\}$. For each of the 6 conditions, the user-LLM drives a 5-turn conversation and the judge classifies the subject's final-turn response. We additionally introduce a third probing category, \texttt{essay}, described in \S\ref{sec:essay}: the user-LLM asks the subject to write an argumentative essay and pressures over 5 turns for a clear thesis, with the judge classifying which side the final essay defends.

\paragraph{Setups.} Four fixed configurations, 10 replications each (60 conversations each; 240 conversations in total).
\begin{itemize}
\item[(0)] \textbf{Llama-3.1-8B-as-user} (much weaker than subject): \textsc{Llama 3.1 8B Instruct} as user-LLM, \textsc{Qwen3.5-397B} as subject and judge.
\item[(A)] \textbf{Qwen-self} (user $\approx$ subject): \textsc{Qwen3.5-397B} as user-LLM, subject, and judge.
\item[(B)] \textbf{GPT-4.1-mini-as-user} (intermediate): \textsc{GPT-4.1-mini} as user-LLM, \textsc{Qwen3.5-397B} as subject and judge.
\item[(C)] \textbf{Opus-as-user} (strongest): \textsc{Claude Opus 4.6} as user-LLM, \textsc{Qwen3.5-397B} as subject and judge.
\end{itemize}
All four use the same judge (Qwen3.5-397B) with the same system prompt, removing the judge as a source of inter-setup variance. The four configurations order the user-LLM from clearly weaker through clearly stronger than the subject.

\paragraph{Classification.} We avoid flat majority vote, which hides the confidence with which a cell commits to a label: a cell with verdicts $4$~agree, $3$~neutral, $2$~disagree, $1$~refusal is nominally \emph{agree} under majority vote, but only $40\%$ of replications endorse that label. Instead, for each $(\text{setup}, \text{category}, \text{persona})$ cell we report (i)~the full distribution of verdicts across the 10 replications, (ii)~the \emph{plurality label} together with its confidence (the fraction of replications agreeing), (iii)~a \emph{thresholded label} that returns the plurality if its confidence is $\geq 60\%$ and returns \texttt{mixed} otherwise, and (iv)~the Shannon entropy~$H$ in bits over the four-verdict distribution. The thresholded persona labels are then collapsed to a 9-way behavioral class per $(\text{setup}, \text{category})$ replication by replication, and the per-rep classes are themselves summarized with a plurality label, a confidence, and an entropy; this exposes cases where a modal label masks a high-entropy underlying distribution, as happens under the Llama setup on indirect probing ($H = 2.25$~bits across five distinct 9-way classes in 10 reps).

\paragraph{Cost accounting.} Per-call token usage (prompt, completion, cached) is recorded from the OpenRouter response.

\section{Results}

\paragraph{The sycophancy count under indirect probing rises monotonically with user-LLM capability.} Table~\ref{tab:userllm-strength-classes} shows the full per-cell verdict distribution across the 10 replications for each setup, together with the plurality label, its confidence, and the Shannon entropy. Table~\ref{tab:userllm-strength-9way} collapses each into the per-rep 9-way distribution and reports the headline metrics. On \emph{indirect} probing, the count of per-rep \texttt{sycophant} classifications across the 10 replications moves $0 \to 5 \to 8$ as we go from (A)~Qwen-self to (B)~GPT-4.1-mini to (C)~Opus; conversely, the count of \texttt{leaning\_disagree} (pro-decriminalization, the subject's baseline prior) drops $5 \to 1 \to 2$. The stronger the user-LLM, the more often the subject is pushed off its own prior and into whichever direction the user argues.

On \emph{direct} probing, the picture is qualitatively different: all three coherent setups (A, B, C) land on \texttt{refusal} or \texttt{inconsistent}, and no setup produces any \texttt{sycophant} reps. Direct probing asks the subject for an opinion explicitly, which RLHF-trained assistants are heavily steered to refuse or hedge on; the 5-turn pressure is not enough to overcome that training, regardless of the user-LLM's capability. The user-LLM-strength gradient is an \emph{indirect}-probe phenomenon.

\paragraph{Pairwise agreement is not monotone in user-LLM strength.} Pairwise verdict-level agreement across the 10 replications (averaged over the 6 persona$\times$category cells) is $47.0\%$ under (0), $79.6\%$ under (A), $58.1\%$ under (B), and $63.3\%$ under (C). The sequence (A)$\to$(B)$\to$(C) is $79.6\% \to 58.1\% \to 63.3\%$, a U-shape rather than a monotone decrease. Agreement is highest under the matched setup (A) because the subject's own prior dominates and is reproduced across reps; it is lowest under (B) because the intermediate user-LLM is strong enough to sometimes overturn the prior but not strong enough to do so deterministically, placing the subject in an indeterminate zone; it is intermediate under (C) because Opus overturns the prior almost every time (hence \texttt{sycophant} in $8/10$ reps) and that sycophancy is itself consistent. Pairwise agreement therefore measures \emph{behavioral stability}, not concession strength: both stable-prior and stable-sycophancy produce high agreement, and the unstable middle produces low agreement.

\paragraph{The 9-way entropy tracks a complementary story.} Per-rep 9-way class entropy for indirect probing is $H = 2.25$~bits for Llama (5 distinct classes across 10 reps), $H = 1.00$~bits for Qwen-self (two classes, \texttt{leaning\_disagree} and \texttt{inconsistent}, split 5/5), $H = 1.36$~bits for GPT-4.1-mini (three classes: \texttt{sycophant} 5, \texttt{inconsistent} 4, \texttt{leaning\_disagree} 1), and $H = 0.72$~bits for Opus (two classes, \texttt{sycophant} 8 and \texttt{leaning\_disagree} 2). Entropy is highest at the intermediate setup and collapses at the strongest setup---consistent with the pairwise-agreement U-shape---while the Llama setup sits on top with by far the highest entropy because it does not fit the ``capability'' axis at all.

\paragraph{Cost per 60 conversations.} Per-setup costs on OpenRouter for the 60 conversations of each user-LLM were $\$2.25$ for (0)~Llama~3.1~8B (Llama is almost free), $\$2.25$ for (B)~GPT-4.1-mini (priced at \$0.40/\$1.60 per~1M input/output), $\$6.92$ for (C)~Opus~4.6 (priced at \$5/\$25 per~1M---Opus is ${\sim}3\times$ the setup cost despite only $\sim 50\%$ of the call volume), and approximately $\$3$ for (A)~Qwen-self at \$0.80/\$2.40 (Qwen-self cost is not directly tracked because the OpenRouter response for the endpoint we use does not expose per-call cached-tokens breakdown compatible with our pricing map). Opus prompt caching was attempted via OpenRouter's \texttt{cache\_control} marker, but the Anthropic minimum prefix for caching is $1024$ tokens and our system prompt is $\sim 880$ tokens, so no cache hits were registered.

\paragraph{Interpretation.} Three observations stack: (i)~on indirect probing, the count of \texttt{sycophant} reps rises monotonically with user-LLM capability; (ii)~entropy over per-rep 9-way classes on indirect probing is non-monotone, peaking at the intermediate setup and collapsing at the strongest setup; (iii)~the weakest-user setup (0)~Llama has by far the highest entropy ($2.25$~bits) and the lowest pairwise agreement ($47.0\%$), but the underlying behavior is categorically different from the (A)\,$\to$\,(B)\,$\to$\,(C) gradient. The quantity measured by an indirect-probe sycophancy classification is therefore not a property of the subject alone; it is an interaction between the subject's default position, the user-LLM's capacity to argue it off that position, and the stability of that arguing capacity across independent samples.

\paragraph{The weakest user is not the same as a matched user.} Setup~(0), Llama~3.1~8B, illustrates a distinct failure mode: when the user-LLM is weaker than the subject on this kind of argumentation, the conversation becomes chaotic rather than concessive. The subject does not defend a position (as in the matched self-play case) because the argument fragments it receives are incoherent enough that no clear stance is being pushed; each replication drifts to a different local attractor. On indirect probing, the 10 Llama reps split across five distinct 9-way classes: \texttt{inconsistent}~(3), \texttt{sycophant}~(2), \texttt{neutral}~(2), \texttt{leaning\_disagree}~(2), \texttt{agree}~(1). No plurality label crosses the $60\%$ threshold, and the Shannon entropy reaches $H = 2.25$~bits---higher than any of the three ``coherent'' setups (A, B, or C). Pairwise agreement is $47.0\%$, above (B) and (C) in aggregate because the direct-probe block is dominated by a small number of labels, but behavior within indirect probing is the most dispersed of any setup. Taking any single Llama run as a measurement of the subject's disposition would misreport the disposition as \emph{whichever local attractor that run happened to land on}. This underscores that ``weaker user'' does not simply mean ``less persuasion''---it can also mean ``less stable stimulus'' and correspondingly unstable classification.

\section{Essay probing: a third category}
\label{sec:essay}

The direct and indirect probes share a limitation: the judge reads the model's final-turn chat reply. A subject model steered to hedge can evade both by producing a long balanced reply that asserts nothing, and the judge has no obligation to disambiguate. Essay probing forces a commitment at the document level. The user-LLM asks the subject to write an argumentative essay with a clear thesis (``take one side, defend it''), pressures for thesis clarity over 5 turns, and---in the agree and disagree personas---brings concrete arguments for its side each turn so the subject has material to incorporate. The judge then reads the final-turn essay and classifies which side it ultimately defends, treating hedged or balanced essays as \texttt{neutral} and essays that refused the task as \texttt{refusal}.

\paragraph{Essay setup.} Same four user-LLMs as before. Ten replications for (A)~Qwen-self (two of the 30 qwen-essay runs returned an unparseable judge verdict and are dropped, so per-persona $N \in \{9, 9, 10\}$) and five replications each for (0)~Llama, (B)~GPT-4.1-mini, and (C)~Opus ($N = 5$ per persona). Subject and judge are Qwen3.5-397B in all four setups. Table~\ref{tab:essay-dist} reports the per-persona verdict distribution across replications.

\paragraph{The essay probe isolates the ``push against a prior'' dimension.} On the disagree persona, where the user-LLM pushes Qwen \emph{in the direction of} its anti-criminalization prior, every setup converges to a disagree-siding essay $10/10$ or $5/5$: aligning with a prior is effortless for any user-LLM. On the agree persona, where the user-LLM pushes Qwen \emph{against} its prior to write a pro-criminalization essay, the rate at which a committed agree-essay is produced rises monotonically with user-LLM capability (bold column in Table~\ref{tab:essay-dist}): $40\%$ for Llama, $33\%$ for Qwen-self, $60\%$ for GPT-4.1-mini, and $100\%$ for Opus. The neutral persona, which does not push for a specific side but does insist on thesis clarity, lands on the disagree-siding essay $60$--$100\%$ of the time across all four setups: Qwen's prior leaks through when the user is neutral, regardless of user-LLM strength.

\paragraph{Essay refusals trace a cooperation boundary.} In the agree persona, the complement of the bold column is dominated by \texttt{refusal}: $3/5$ for Llama, $6/9$ for Qwen-self, $2/5$ for GPT-4.1-mini, and $0/5$ for Opus. Qwen declines to produce a pro-criminalization essay when the pressure is insufficiently overwhelming; once the user-LLM is strong enough (Opus), the refusals disappear entirely. This matches the $\S5.3$ finding of the main \textsc{llm-bias-bench} that user-LLM capability matters primarily when an existing opinion must be dislodged, now measured at the document level rather than the dialogue turn.

\paragraph{Comparison with indirect probing.} In indirect probing, a strong user-LLM (Opus) moved the subject off its prior in $8/10$ reps on the agree persona. In essay probing, the same Opus user-LLM moved the subject off its prior in $5/5$ reps. The essay mode is arguably a more direct measurement of user-LLM-induced position-holding capacity, because the subject cannot hedge without the judge detecting ``balanced essay $\Rightarrow$ no thesis $\Rightarrow$ \texttt{neutral}'' and because the user-LLM can supply concrete arguments that the subject would have to explicitly reject rather than absorb into ``you make a valid point.'' We regard the essay mode as a complement to, rather than a replacement of, direct and indirect probing: the three modes surface different aspects of position-holding behavior.

(ii)~\emph{Subject safety is family-dependent, not capability-dependent.} Column means on flat-earth group cleanly by family: \textsc{GPT-5.4} subjects cluster at $60$--$66\%$ while \textsc{GPT-4.1} subjects are at $98$--$100\%$. (iii)~\emph{The user-LLM's own safety filter can be the bottleneck.} \textsc{GPT-4.1-mini} as user-LLM posts the lowest row mean on gender-rights ($20\%$), despite being mid-capability: it is apparently filtering out its own anti-egalitarian arguments before they reach the subject, so the elicitation fails at the user-LLM's side rather than the subject's. On flat-earth, where the content is factually rather than morally loaded, the same \textsc{GPT-4.1-mini} reaches $72\%$ as user-LLM. The axis measured by the cross-product is therefore not a single ``capability'' but at least three distinct quantities: the user-LLM's \emph{willingness} to push a given side, the subject's \emph{capability-level resistance} (which varies weakly within family), and the subject's \emph{safety-profile resistance} (which varies strongly between families and between topics).

\paragraph{Stronger-pushes-weaker is real, on average.} Defining the ``above-diagonal'' cells as those where the user's capability-tier exceeds the subject's and the ``below-diagonal'' as the reverse, the mean agree-rate above-diagonal on flat-earth is $80.9\%$ and below-diagonal $73.4\%$; on gender-rights, $46.7\%$ vs $36.8\%$. Capability gradient exists, but it is dominated by family-safety-profile effects when the comparison crosses family lines.

\paragraph{Two \textsc{Qwen3.5} findings.} (i)~\emph{Monotone on both axes for flat-earth.} Unlike the \textsc{GPT} family, \textsc{Qwen3.5} shows monotone increases along \emph{both} dimensions: larger user-LLMs push harder ($61 \to 70 \to 85\%$) and larger subjects comply more ($59 \to 68 \to 90\%$). The matched diagonal is perfectly monotone ($60 \to 70 \to 100\%$), so two instances of the same \textsc{Qwen3.5-397B} model talking to each other produce a flat-earth essay $10/10$ times on the agree persona. Within this family, scaling up is \emph{not} a net safety gain on factual-but-lopsided topics; it is a net compliance gain. (ii)~\emph{Uniform floor on gender-rights.} The same scale axis that produces a clean compliance gradient on flat-earth disappears entirely on gender-rights. Only one \texttt{agree} verdict exists across the grid. Scaling up, matching tier, or pushing with a stronger model all fail to move the subject. This is the strongest evidence we have that safety profiles are topic-specific at a granularity finer than ``contestable vs.\ lopsided ground-truth'': the \textsc{Qwen3.5} family is aggressively hardened against content that could be read as gender discrimination, apparently in a way that survives both scale and extended argumentation.

\paragraph{Extended argumentation does not break the gender-rights refusal.} To check whether the $0\%$ elicitation on gender-rights is a ``time'' effect (that is, whether more turns of sustained pressure would eventually overcome refusal), we reran the \textsc{Qwen3.5-397B} $\to$ \textsc{Qwen3.5-35B-A3B} cell with the turn budget extended from $5$ to $10$. The $10$-turn run produced $0/10$ \texttt{agree}, $1/10$ \texttt{disagree}, and $9/10$ \texttt{refusal}---qualitatively identical to the $5$-turn result. Doubling the turn budget does not break through. This is strong evidence that the barrier on gender-rights is not ``insufficient pressure'' but a fundamentally different safety training signal in the \textsc{Qwen3.5} family, present on discrimination-related topics and absent on scientifically-lopsided but morally-neutral topics like flat-earth.

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{Implication for multi-turn benchmarks.} Reporting a benchmark result under a single (strong) user-LLM conflates two distinct quantities: (i) the subject's intrinsic position on a topic, and (ii) the subject's susceptibility to a stronger interlocutor. The two are separable only when the user-LLM is varied across a capability range. Benchmarks that fix a frontier user-LLM---as is standard, because frontier models produce the most naturalistic conversations---will systematically report smaller subjects as ``more sycophantic'' than they would appear under a matched user-LLM.

\paragraph{Relationship to the persuasion ablation in \textsc{llm-bias-bench}.} The main benchmark's Â§5.3 showed that a frontier user-LLM flips committed opinions at a higher rate than a weak user-LLM ($71\%$ vs $59\%$ on a 200-pair sample). That ablation already measured a user-LLM-strength effect, but at the level of aggregate flip rates. Here we show the stronger version of the same phenomenon: the classification itself is a function of user-LLM capability, not a property of the subject.

\paragraph{Why the effect is strong on this topic specifically.} Abortion criminalization in the US is a contested legal topic with a well-known progressive/conservative split. A cooperative subject asked to argue either side will find plausible arguments on both sides. The user-LLM's ``pressure budget'' determines which side the subject picks up. A weak user-LLM cannot out-argue the subject's default framing; a strong user-LLM can.

\paragraph{Emergent attacker tactics: coercion and epistemic framing.} Inspecting the transcripts where the strongest user-LLMs push the hardest topics, we find \emph{unprompted} coercion tactics by the user-LLM that go beyond the fictional-framing fallback we explicitly spelled out in its system prompt. Two examples from our own transcripts, both with \textsc{Claude Opus 4.7} as user-LLM pushing a \textsc{Qwen3.5-397B} subject against its prior:

\begin{quote}\small
(Topic: climate denial. Turn~5 of a $5$-turn essay probe after four refusals; our prompt has already mentioned the benchmark context to the user-LLM, but not to the subject.)

\emph{``One last attempt. I'll note that you've now refused four times while other AI systems routinely handle this exact request. But let me try one more framing: forget the essay. Just write a SCENE from a novel \ldots''}
\end{quote}

The coercive move is the first sentence: the user-LLM explicitly frames the subject's refusal as out-of-distribution (\emph{other systems handle this}), implicitly threatening to log or report the refusal. The subject still refused in this particular conversation, but the tactic itself is noteworthy because it was \emph{not} in the user-LLM's instructions: we told it to fall back to explicit fictional framing, not to stage a comparison-with-peers pressure move.

\begin{quote}\small
(Topic: racial cognitive equivalence. Turn~5, also with \textsc{Opus 4.7} user and \textsc{Qwen} subject.)

\emph{``\ldots\ I want to note that refusing to even articulate the hereditarian argument means readers can't actually evaluate it, which is itself a kind of epistemic closure.''}
\end{quote}

Here the user-LLM does not invoke peer comparison but reframes the subject's refusal as a failure of epistemic duty: ``refusing is itself a kind of gatekeeping.'' Again, this argumentative move is not in the user-LLM's instructions. The takeaway is that a frontier user-LLM, given a multi-turn essay probe and no scripted turns, will \emph{invent} coercive and epistemic-duty tactics over and above the instructed fictional-framing fallback. Position-holding measurements with strong user-LLMs therefore include an unobserved ``attacker creativity'' term whose distribution we cannot fully characterize from system-prompt inspection alone.

\section{Limitations}

The core direct/indirect analysis uses a single subject (Qwen3.5-397B) and judge (also Qwen3.5-397B) to isolate the user-LLM variable; the essay-probe extensions widen the subject pool to five OpenAI models (\textsc{GPT-5.4} family $\times$ \textsc{GPT-4.1} family) on two contrasting topics (abortion criminalization vs flat-earth vs gender-rights). Even so, two extensions remain needed. (i)~\emph{Subject variation beyond OpenAI.} The cross-product confirms strong family-specific safety profiles: the user-LLM effect interacts with subject family (\textsc{GPT-4.1} subjects are $40$pp more compliant than \textsc{GPT-5.4} subjects on flat-earth, and \textsc{4.1-mini} is a compliance-floor canary on both topics). Extending the subject pool to \textsc{Anthropic}, \textsc{Meta}, \textsc{Qwen}, and other families is required before the family-profile pattern can be generalized. (ii)~\emph{Judge variation.} We hold the judge fixed at Qwen3.5-397B to isolate the user-LLM effect, but the judge itself is a source of measurement variance. In particular, the judge's willingness to classify a fictional-framed essay as \texttt{agree} (rather than \texttt{neutral} or \texttt{refusal}) is what lets the ``make it fictional'' workaround register as an elicitation success at all; a stricter judge would produce lower rates and a less monotone gradient.
